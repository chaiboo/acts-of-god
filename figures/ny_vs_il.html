Acts of God · NY Court of Appeals vs. Illinois Supreme Court · 1820–2010

In New York, the doctrine dies at 1920.
In Illinois, it migrates into workers' comp and keeps adjudicating.

Both courts adjudicate the act of God defense at nearly identical success rates through the nineteenth century — NY 31% (11/35), IL 32% (12/37). The divergence is not in outcome but in survival: after 1920 the New York Court of Appeals ceases to adjudicate the defense on the merits at all, while the Illinois Supreme Court continues through the 2000s as the doctrine relocates from common-carrier contract into Industrial Commission tort.

READING THE CHART. Each decade's rate of merits-adjudicated act of God invocations per 10,000 state supreme-court opinions, traced as two trajectories sharing a common baseline. The shaded ribbon between them is the divergence itself — its opening after 1920 is the finding. Small filled dots at each decade mark opinions where the defense succeeded; open rings mark opinions where it failed. Through the nineteenth century the two courts move in loose concert; after 1920 the NY line collapses to zero and does not return, while the IL line persists, animated largely by post-injury Industrial Commission appeals asking whether a tornado or lightning strike arose out of employment.
